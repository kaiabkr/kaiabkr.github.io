DISPROPORTIONATTE EFFECTS ON MINORITY GROUPS

Gentrified areas see an increase in white non-Hispanic people and non-gentrified low-income areas see an increase
in minority groups. As gentrification persists, minority low-income communities are pushed out of their homes,
ultimately leading to segregation. Large cities such as Detroit, Philadelphia, and areas within New York City have
lots of gentrification. Over the past few years, gentrification has been an increasingly pressing issue
due to the pandemic. This time in history brought attention to the rough policing against people of color.
The more segregation becomes prevelant, the more targeted minority areas become.

A Standford University study observed the disproportionate effects of gentrification on minority groups.
It found that people moving from neighborhoods that are non-predominantly black have more housing options as they are
less likely to face rent discrimination. They specifically found that Black communities face additional constraints
when moving to lead them to move to affordable yet disadvantaged neighborhoods that have less access to public transportation and resources.



EMINENT DOMAIN

One of the main factors advancing gentrification is Eminent Domain. Eminent Domain is the government acquisition of private
property for public use. It pushes modern development in once-underdeveloped areas. Governments often utilize this tactic to
display people of color out of their neighborhoods in the name of urban renewal and modernization. But the issue with Eminent
Domain is that these modern redevelopments increase the cost of living in the area whether that’s property taxes,
the cost of groceries, etc. Between 1949 and 1973, eminent domain displaced one million people… ⅔ of them being African-American. This made black people five times more likely to be displaced than white people. The disproportionate effects of gentrification and eminent domain on minority communities are undeniable. Now we must ask ourselves… how can we solve this issue?
